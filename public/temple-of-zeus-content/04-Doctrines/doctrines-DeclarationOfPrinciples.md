# Declaration of Principles

On Theological Critique, the Distinction Between Doctrine and People, and the Boundaries of Institutional and Private Expression

ANNO MMXXVI   ·   15 March 2026

## Preamble

The Temple of Zeus has existed as a living religious community for over two decades. During these twenty years we have built a body of theology, ritual practice, sacred literature, and spiritual discipline rooted in the worship of the Ancient Gods: the Theoi of Hellas, the Netjeru of Kemet, the Devas of the Vedic tradition, and their counterparts across every civilization that preserved the original spiritual inheritance of humanity. We have done this openly, without concealment, and without apology.

This Declaration is issued from a position of theological maturity, sovereign authority, and the confidence that what we have built surpasses, in intellectual rigour, in spiritual depth, and in moral coherence, the doctrinal systems of the traditions that sought to destroy us. We say this because it is demonstrable. A theology that diagnoses its own pathologies, that names its own potential corruptions, that applies its critical instruments to itself before applying them to others, and that organizes the human relationship with the Divine under Cosmic Law rather than tribal election, is structurally superior to theologies that claim infallibility while practising exclusion. This Declaration makes our position explicit.

We issue this Declaration because precision is a religious duty. The Goddess Ma'at demands accuracy. What is inaccurate is disordered. What is disordered is impious. This Declaration serves Ma'at.

## [#](#article_1)Article I: On the Right to Theological Critique

The Temple of Zeus affirms, without reservation, the absolute right to subject any theological system, religious institution, doctrinal claim, or priestly structure to rigorous critical analysis. This right is among the oldest in human civilization. Socrates exercised it. The philosophers of the Academy exercised it. The authors of the Greek Magical Papyri exercised it. We exercise it.

We reject the modern conceit that religions are beyond criticism. No doctrine is sacred merely because someone believes it. No institution is immune from scrutiny merely because it claims divine authority. No priestly class is above examination merely because it has held power for centuries. The critical examination of religious claims is not hostility toward religion. It is the highest form of respect for the truth that religion claims to serve.

The Temple of Zeus will continue to analyse, critique, and where warranted, oppose theological doctrines and institutional practices that we judge to be spiritually harmful, historically fraudulent, or destructive to the relationship between humanity and the Divine. This is a core function of our theology and we will not abandon it.

## [#](#article_2)Article II: On the Distinction Between Doctrine and People

The Temple of Zeus draws an absolute and non-negotiable distinction between the critique of a theological system and hostility toward the people born into that system.

A human being does not choose the religion into which they are born. A child raised within any tradition inherits its doctrines before possessing the capacity to evaluate them. To condemn a person for the religion of their birth is to condemn them for something over which they had no agency. This is theologically imprecise, strategically foolish, and beneath the standard of Ma'at. The individual is not the institution. The institution may be diseased; the individual may yet be healthy, or may yet be healed. Our critique is aimed at structures, not at souls born under those structures.

Our theological vocabulary reflects this distinction with precision:

-   When we speak of [Yehuborim,](../liturgical_terms_yehubor.php) we speak of a spiritual pathology: the condition of bearing a divine seal while being emptied of divine content. We do not speak of a people. Any priest, of any tradition, including our own, can embody this pathology.
-   When we speak of [Birburim,](../liturgical_terms_birburim.php) we speak of corrupt sacred speech: the eleven modes by which holy language is weaponized. We do not speak of a language or a nation. Birburim can be uttered in Greek, in Hebrew, in Arabic, in English, or in any tongue.
-   When we speak of [Sahiburah,](../liturgical_terms_sahiburah.php) we speak of crimes against the divine body. We do not speak of a specific priesthood by ethnicity, but of a type of priestly corruption that has appeared in every civilization that has ever had priests.
-   When we speak of [Izfet,](../liturgical_terms_izfet.php) we speak of the entropy of the sacred: the cosmic disorder that results when any of the above pathologies is left unchecked. Its opposite, Ma'at, is the sovereign law of the cosmos. It is not the property of any single tradition, nor is it distributed equally among those who ignore it. Ma'at must be earned through alignment. It is not given as a birthright.

Any reading of our theological vocabulary that reduces these terms to ethnic slurs is a misreading. We reject it categorically. We have built these terms with dual etymologies (Greek and Semitic), with historical documentation, with isopsephic verification, and with explicit counter-terms (Theophoros, Hierologia, Diaugeia, Sahu Katharos, and the rest), precisely so that the system is self-evidently analytical rather than polemical.

## [#](#article_3)Article III: On What We Reject

The Temple of Zeus formally and irrevocably rejects the following. We state these rejections from strength, from clarity, and from the obligation that Ma'at places upon those who serve Her: to name what is false with the same precision with which we name what is true.

### III.I — On Race, Diversity, and the Rejection of Both Supremacy and False Universalism

#### III.I.a — The Reality of Difference

The Temple of Zeus rejects both poles of the modern debate on human difference, because both are lies.

Race exists. It is an observable fact of nature, written in biology, in morphology, in the diversity of the human species as it adapted across continents and millennia. The differences between peoples are real, beautiful, and worth preserving. True diversity is not the homogenization of all peoples into an undifferentiated mass; it is the flourishing of distinct peoples, cultures, languages, and traditions, each contributing their irreplaceable note to the symphony of human civilization. We do not seek a world in which all difference is erased. We seek a world in which difference is honoured without being weaponized.

Not all civilizations are at the same stage of development. Not all nations have achieved the same material, institutional, or cultural attainments. To pretend otherwise is not virtue; it is dishonesty. The Temple of Zeus does not traffic in comfortable fictions. We observe the world as it is, not as political ideology wishes it to be.

> We seek a world in which difference is honoured without being weaponized.

#### III.I.b — The Rejection of Supremacy

What we reject, absolutely and without qualification, is the conversion of natural diversity into a doctrine of supremacy: the claim that biological difference confers spiritual rank, that the Gods favour one bloodline over another, or that any people has the right to subjugate, exclude, or diminish another on the basis of birth. This claim, wherever it appears, is a theological pathology. It is existentially dangerous to the future of the human species, because it converts the natural order of diversity into a permanent engine of conflict.

> The conversion of natural diversity into a doctrine of supremacy is the disease. The order of Ma'at is its cure.

#### III.I.c — The Gods and the Nations

The Ancient Gods created all peoples and are worshipped, under diverse names and in diverse forms, by all peoples across every continent: as Zeus in Hellas, as Atum in Kemet, as Indra in the Vedic world, as Perun among the Slavs, as Shango and Nzazi in Africa, as Shangdi in China. The Gods did not create diversity in order to establish permanent domination of one people over another. They created diversity so that each people would develop its own strengths, its own wisdom, and its own contribution to the order of the cosmos. Natural differences in ability, in development, in attainment exist and are acknowledged. What does not exist is a divine mandate granting any single people permanent sovereignty over the rest. A tradition that spans every race, every continent, and every civilization cannot coherently serve racial supremacy, nor can it coherently serve the denial of race. It serves Ma'at: the ordered recognition of what is real, including both the unity of the species and the diversity within it.

> The Gods did not choose one tribe. The Gods fathered all tribes, each with its own gifts, its own calling, and its own dignity.

#### III.I.d — The Doctrine of "Chosenness" as Theological Pathology

We note, with theological precision, that the doctrine of inherent ethnic or spiritual superiority is not a fringe position in world religion. It is a central, codified tenet of certain dominant traditions: the doctrine that one particular people is "chosen" above all others, that the remainder of humanity exists in a category of spiritual subordination, and that this hierarchy is divinely ordained and eternal. We name this doctrine for what it is: a theological pathology. We reject it because it threatens the ordered coexistence of the human family and contradicts the universality of the Divine as attested by every ancient civilization on earth.

Where institutions or communities have built their entire theological identity upon this claim of exclusive election, and have sustained it across centuries as an instrument of separation, exclusion, and spiritual supremacy, they have manifested the condition our theology names Yehubor: the bearing of a divine seal emptied of its universal content, turned inward to serve the elevation of the self at the expense of the whole. We state this as a diagnosis of a doctrine, applied with the same precision we apply to all doctrines, including our own. The doctrine is the disease. The people are its first victims.

> All peoples, accepted. All differences, respected. All organized under Ma'at. No claim of divine supremacy, from any quarter, tolerated.

### III.II — On the Weaponization of the Word "Antisemitism"

We acknowledge that the history behind this term contains real suffering. We can see why the topic is sensitive, and we are capable of distinguishing between genuine ethnic persecution, which has occurred and which we oppose, and the instrumentalization of that history as a censorship device, which is what concerns us here.

The word "antisemitism" has undergone a transformation that its original usage would scarcely recognize. What began as a description of ethnic hatred has been systematically expanded into a mechanism by which an entire theological tradition, an entire institutional apparatus, and an entire political programme have placed themselves in a category beyond criticism. The accusation no longer requires evidence of hatred. It requires only that someone has questioned a doctrine, examined a historical claim, or critiqued an institutional practice associated with a particular religious tradition. The emotional charge of the word is deployed precisely to prevent the rational examination that would reveal whether the accusation has substance. This is not protection. It is insulation. And insulation from critique is the defining characteristic of every institution that has something to hide.

We observe plainly that the deployment of this word has become increasingly detached from any rational standard. It is levelled at scholars for citing historical sources. It is levelled at theologians for examining doctrines of election. It is levelled at political analysts for questioning the actions of a state. It is levelled at anyone, anywhere, who declines to treat one particular tradition as occupying a sacred category above the reach of the human intellect. In practice, the word has become an empty emotional accusation designed to produce silence through intimidation rather than agreement through argument. It presupposes that one tradition is "beyond criticism," a category that Ma'at does not recognize and that the Temple of Zeus will never accept for any tradition, including our own.

Our position is straightforward: we critique theological doctrines and institutional practices on their merits. We extend the same scrutiny to Christianity, to Islam, to Rabbinic Judaism, and to ourselves. We will not grant any tradition a special exemption from examination, because exemption from examination is the first step toward the entrenchment of Yehubor. Every tradition that has ever claimed to be beyond question has used that claim to conceal corruption. We will not participate in that pattern, and we will not be silenced by those who demand that we do.

> Exemption from examination is the first step toward the entrenchment of Yehubor. We grant it to no tradition, including our own.

### III.III — On the Doctrine of Supremacy and the Open Door

The Temple of Zeus does not bar any person from its doors on the basis of their political history or prior ideological commitments. We are a religious institution, not a political tribunal. A person who arrives in sincerity, willing to learn, is welcome regardless of where they have been. However, we state with absolute clarity that the theology of the Ancient Gods is structurally incompatible with every ideology of supremacy. This includes, without distinction or ranking: National Socialism and neo-Nazism, which deify race; Zionism in its theological-supremacist form, which deifies ethnic election; Christian Dominionism, which deifies institutional control; Islamist theocracy, which deifies juridical submission; and every other system that elevates one category of human being above another by divine mandate. These ideologies are theologically false. The Ancient Gods were never the gods of a single race, a single nation, or a single "chosen" lineage. The same Zeus worshipped in Athens was worshipped as Amun in Thebes, as Baal Zebul in Canaan, as Nzazi in the Congo, as Shangdi in the Middle Kingdom. A person who enters the Temple carrying the ideology of supremacy will find that the theology itself dissolves it, because the Gods do not confirm the fantasy that any tribe stands above the others in the eyes of Heaven. We do not expel such a person. We teach them. If they cannot learn, they will leave of their own accord, because the truth will become intolerable to their pride.

> We do not expel. We teach. If they cannot learn, the truth itself will expel them.

### III.IV — On Pseudo-Occultism

The Left Hand Path, in its legitimate forms, is a tradition of spiritual sovereignty. It is not a licence for cruelty, degeneracy, or the aestheticization of evil. Those who use the language of the occult to dress up bigotry, glorify violence, or indulge in transgression without spiritual substance will find nothing here to nourish them. The Gods are not interested in edginess. They are interested in truth.

> The Gods are not interested in edginess. They are interested in truth.

### III.V — On Violence and the Abrahamic Precedent

#### III.V.a — The Historical Record

The three religions that currently dominate the earth, Christianity, Islam, and Judaism, were not spread by the persuasive beauty of their doctrines alone. They were spread by the sword, by the fire, by the forced conversion, by the destruction of temples, by the murder of priests, by the erasure of libraries, and by the systematic extermination of every competing spiritual tradition they encountered.

The Crusades. The Inquisition. The Islamic conquests. The destruction of the Serapeum. The forced conversions of Saxons and Slavs. The Reconquista. The witch trials. The pogroms conducted by all sides against all others. These are not aberrations of the Abrahamic tradition. They are its operational history.

> Billions of adherents were acquired not through enlightenment but through Varvarim: the wars of spiritual imposition, waged by institutions steeped in Yehubor.

#### III.V.b — The Cycle of Ruin

We name this history from precision. It happened. It is documented. And it reveals a pattern that is deeper than any single religion's guilt.

The entire historical narrative of the Abrahamic world is a narrative of building upon the ruins of the previous occupant. Judaism built its temple upon the high places of the Canaanites. Christianity built its churches upon the foundations of pagan temples, its calendar upon pagan festivals, its saints upon pagan gods, and its institutional power upon the ashes of the Roman civic religion it displaced. Islam built its mosques upon the churches of the Christians and the temples of the Zoroastrians, its conquests explicitly modeled upon the precedent set by the previous two. And when these three turned upon one another, the pattern continued: Crusade and counter-Crusade, pogrom and expulsion, sectarian war and counter-reformation, each building its righteousness upon the rubble of the last.

This is not a sequence of unrelated events. It is a single, self-perpetuating cycle: the Cycle of Varvarim. Each successor tradition justified its violence by pointing to the violence of its predecessor. Each claimed that its destruction was holy because the thing destroyed was unholy. Each was, in turn, destroyed or diminished by the next claimant operating under the identical logic. The cycle produces nothing permanent. It produces only ruins upon which the next conqueror builds, until he too is conquered.

The Temple of Zeus enters this history with one aim: to end the cycle. We will never convert by force, never suppress by violence, never build our congregation upon the ashes of another's temple. We do not seek to replace the Abrahamic institutions by destroying them. We seek to render them obsolete by building something that is true. The destruction, if it comes, will come from within those institutions, as it always does when a structure can no longer sustain the weight of its own contradictions. Our task is to be standing, intact and ready, when that collapse occurs, offering an alternative that was built upon Ma'at rather than upon the bones of the conquered.

> We do not seek to replace the Abrahamic institutions by destroying them. We seek to render them obsolete by building something that is true.

#### III.V.c — Our Weapons

Our weapons are theological, liturgical, and legal. We build, we write, we argue, we litigate, and we worship. This is the discipline of a tradition that proves its truth by the quality of its doctrine, the depth of its practice, and the calibre of its adherents. Those who mistake this discipline for vulnerability will discover, should they test us, that our restraint is strategic, our legal architecture is formidable, and our resolve is absolute.

> Our restraint is strategic, our legal architecture is formidable, and our resolve is absolute.

## [#](#article_4)Article IV: On What We Do Not Renounce

This Declaration is an assertion of position. Here is what we hold, what we will always hold, and what no force on earth will cause us to relinquish:

-   **The right to identify and name spiritual pathologies wherever they occur.** The Corpus of Theological Pathology is a diagnostic tool without parallel in any modern religious tradition. Where other traditions moralize, we diagnose. Where other traditions condemn sins while committing them institutionally, we name the structural conditions that produce the sins. We will continue to develop this system, apply it, and teach it. It is among the most important contributions our tradition has made to the theology of the human species.
-   **The right to criticize religious institutions that claim monopoly over the Divine.** Any institution that asserts that it alone possesses divine truth, that all other paths are false, and that its authority supersedes the conscience of the individual, will be subjected to our critique. This includes, without exception, institutional forms within Christianity, Rabbinic Judaism, and Islam. It also includes our own tradition, against which we have built explicit safeguards: the Corpus of Theological Pathology applies to us with the same force it applies to others, the doctrine of Ma'at demands self-examination before external judgement, and the entire framework of Yehubor, Birburim, Atibilibil, and their counterparts was constructed with the explicit recognition that these pathologies are universal, not exclusive to any single tradition. A religion that cannot diagnose its own diseases has no authority to diagnose the diseases of others. We have built the diagnostic instrument. We apply it first to ourselves. This is what separates a living theology from a dead institution.
-   **The right to political thought and political expression.** Religion does not exist in a political vacuum. When political power is wielded in the name of theological claims, we will speak. When laws are passed to suppress the worship of the Ancient Gods, we will oppose them. When religious lobbies seek to impose their doctrines upon civil society, we will resist. The separation of temple and state is a principle we affirm; the silencing of the temple by the state is a tyranny we reject.
-   **The right to practice the liturgical arts of binding and restoration.** Every major religion possesses traditions of imprecation, excommunication, anathema, and spiritual sanction. Christianity has its anathema sit. Islam has its la'na. Judaism has its cherem, its Pulsa deNura, its Karet. These traditions are documented, historical, and practiced to this day. Our own Corpus of Curses exists within this universal lineage, rooted in the Greek Magical Papyri and the liturgical practices of the ancient Graeco-Egyptian world. The critical difference is this: our imprecatory tradition is directed at spiritual pathologies, at conditions and structures, not at persons by virtue of their birth, their blood, or their ethnicity. Where the imprecatory traditions of the dominant religions have historically been wielded against races, against nations, against entire peoples branded as enemies of God by accident of birth, ours targets the pathology itself, wherever it manifests, in whomever it manifests, including in our own. This distinction is fundamental. It is the difference between a weapon of Ma'at and a weapon of Izfet. We preserve the former and reject the latter.
-   **The right to declare the Ancient Gods living and sovereign.** The Gods are not myths. They are not metaphors. They are not psychological archetypes. They are not cultural relics for academic study. They are not the defeated pantheon of a superseded age. They are Gods: older than every institution that claimed to replace Them, more enduring than every empire that tried to erase Them, and more present in the lived experience of Their worshippers than all the theological arguments constructed to deny Them. Zeus lives. Athena lives. Atum lives. Shiva lives. They lived before the Abrahamic traditions existed, They persisted through two millennia of suppression, and They will be worshipped long after the institutions that sought to bury Them have crumbled into the dust from which they came. This claim will offend those who believe their God is the only one. We do not care. Truth is not subject to the comfort of its audience.

## [#](#article_5)Article V: On the Separation of Institutional and Private Expression

The Temple of Zeus distinguishes between two spheres of expression:

Institutional expression is the official voice of the Temple: its published theology, its liturgical texts, its declarations, its website, and any communication issued in the name of the Temple of Zeus. This expression is bound by the principles articulated in this Declaration. It is theological, precise, documented, and subject to the standards of Ma'at.

Private expression is the personal voice of individual members, including the leadership. A member of the Temple of Zeus, speaking in their personal capacity, retains the full right to free speech as guaranteed by the laws of their jurisdiction. The Temple does not police the private opinions of its members, nor does it accept responsibility for them.

When a member speaks, the question is simple: are they speaking as the Temple, or as themselves? If the Temple's name, symbols, or authority are invoked, they speak as the Temple and are bound by these Principles. If they speak in their own name, they speak for themselves alone.

This is a structural principle, deliberately designed. The Temple is a religious institution with theological standards. Its members are free human beings with the right to their own thought. Both are honoured. Both are maintained. This is the natural consequence of a tradition that values both order and liberty.

## [#](#article_6)Article VI: On Politics

The Temple of Zeus rejects no person on the basis of their political convictions, from any point on the political spectrum. We are a religious institution, not a political party, and our doors are not filtered by ideology.

However, we regard politics, in its entirety, as a domain of limited and temporary perception. Political movements arise from the necessities of the moment: from economic pressure, from social grievance, from the struggle for power within a given generation. They are responses to conditions. They are not truth itself. They shift with the century, with the decade, with the news cycle. What was radical becomes orthodox; what was orthodox becomes heretical. The entire apparatus rotates without arriving.

For this reason, the Temple of Zeus does not ground itself in political ideology. We ground ourselves in Eternal Culture and Cosmic Law: in Ma'at, in Satya, in the sovereignty of Zeus. These do not shift with the election cycle. They do not reverse with the change of regime. They are the constants against which every political claim can be measured, and by which every political upheaval can be understood without being consumed by it.

We promote reason, especially in times of political upheaval and injustice, precisely because political upheaval is the environment in which reason is most easily abandoned. When the world demands that you choose a side immediately, the Temple counsels that you think first and choose from principle rather than from panic. When political discourse descends into the mutual exchange of Birburim, the Temple offers the counter-discipline of Hierologia: speech grounded in truth rather than in tribal allegiance.

Members of the Temple hold diverse political views. They are entitled to them. The Temple itself holds no political platform. It holds something older and more durable: a theological framework within which political questions can be examined without idolatry, without hysteria, and without the surrender of the soul to the passions of the hour.

## [#](#article_7)Article VII: On Our History

The Temple of Zeus has existed as a community for over twenty years. We state our history openly, because those who hide their past become prisoners of it, while those who own it become its masters.

The community that became the Temple of Zeus originated within the Left Hand Path tradition known as Spiritual Satanism. The founding premise of that tradition was not the worship of a Christian devil. It was the recognition that the entities called "demons" by the Abrahamic religions were, in historical fact, the demonized Gods of older civilizations. "Satan" was a title applied to adversaries of the new monotheistic order. "Lucifer" was Eosphoros, the Light-Bearer, a Graeco-Roman divine epithet. "Beelzebub" was Ba'al Zebul, the Lord of the High Place. "Astaroth" was Astarte, who is Aphrodite. The "demons" of the grimoire tradition were the Theoi of Hellas, the Netjeru of Kemet, and the Ilu of Mesopotamia, stripped of their names, their rites, and their dignity by a theological programme of cultural inversion.

This premise was, and remains, historically defensible. The demonization of pagan deities by Abrahamic institutions is not a conspiracy theory; it is a documented fact of religious history, attested in patristic literature, medieval demonology, and modern academic scholarship. To name this process is not paranoia. It is history.

However, the tradition as it existed had critical flaws. It operated within the inverted frame of its enemies: it used the names given by the demonizers rather than the original names of the Gods. It called Zeus "Satan." It called Aphrodite "Astaroth." In doing so, it inadvertently perpetuated the very inversion it claimed to oppose. It also attracted, as such movements inevitably do, individuals and factions whose interest was not in the Gods but in provocation, in political extremism, and in the aesthetic of transgression for its own sake. These elements had no genuine spiritual vocation. They used the language of the Left Hand Path to clothe hatred, ignorance, and the lowest forms of human discourse in a mask of esoteric authority.

The transformation was this: the current leadership inherited this community and undertook to do what should have been done from the beginning: to strip away the inverted frame entirely and to restore the Gods to their proper names, their proper rites, and their proper theology. We ceased to call Zeus "Satan" and called Him Zeus. We ceased to use the grimoire names and returned to the Hellenic, Egyptian, and Graeco-Egyptian sources. We built a theology rooted not in opposition to Christianity but in the positive reality of the Ancient Gods as living, sovereign, divine beings. We developed the liturgical corpus, the system of theurgy, the Corpus of Theological Pathology, the isopsephic tradition, the sacred geometry, the reconstruction of the divine names from the Greek Magical Papyri and the Hekhalot-Merkavah literature. The foundation was laid by those who came before. The edifice is ours.

In the course of this transformation, the political extremists, the pseudo-occultists, and the hateful were expelled or departed of their own accord. They had no interest in actual theology, and theology is all we do. The Temple of Zeus as it exists today bears the same relationship to its Spiritual Satanist origins as Christianity bears to Second Temple Judaism: it grew from that soil, it acknowledges that soil, and it has become something that the soil alone could not have produced.

Our origins require no apology. The recognition that the Ancient Gods were demonized was correct. The impulse to restore Them was correct. What was incorrect was the method: using the vocabulary of your enemy to name your own Gods is a form of continued captivity. We have ended that captivity. The Gods are called by Their names. The rites are performed in Their languages. The theology is built on Their foundations, on the positive reality of what the Gods are, rather than on the negation of what Their enemies claim.

There exist, in the archives of the internet, expressions from earlier periods of our community's life that reflect the rawness, the imprecision, and in some cases the undisciplined hostility of the environment from which we emerged. We acknowledge this openly, because transparency is a prerequisite of authority. We grew beyond it, because the Gods required it. Ma'at does not accept imprecision as a permanent condition. Every living tradition either refines itself or decays. The Catholic Church took two thousand years to produce Nostra Aetate. We took twenty. The measure of a tradition is its capacity to refine itself without losing its core, and its willingness to subject itself to the same fire it directs at others. We have both. The core, the worship of the Ancient Gods and the opposition to Their demonization, has never changed. Everything else has been subjected to the fire of Ma'at, and what could not survive that fire was not worth preserving.

This Declaration constitutes the definitive statement of our Principles as of the date of its publication. All prior expressions that contradict this Declaration are hereby superseded. The Temple of Zeus speaks with one voice, and this is that voice.

## [#](#article_8)Article VIII: Standing Invitation

The Temple of Zeus does not recruit. It does not proselytize. It does not knock on doors. It does not beg for adherents. It does not lower its standards to fill its ranks.

Those who seek the Ancient Gods and who are willing to live under Ma'at (Cosmic Law of Truth), Satya (Eternal Truth), and Zeus (Sovereign of the Universe) may approach the Temple regardless of their race, their ethnicity, their nation, or their prior allegiances. The door is not barred by blood. It is not barred by tongue. It is not barred by continent. The same Gods who speak as Zeus in Hellas speak as Atum in Kemet, as Indra in the Vedic lands, as Nzazi in Africa, as Shangdi in the East.

But let there be no misunderstanding: the door is barred by sincerity and by capacity. The Temple exists for those with genuine spiritual vocation: those willing to study, to discipline themselves, to submit to self-examination, and to be corrected by truth rather than comforted by opinion. The spiritually idle, the doctrinally promiscuous, and the merely curious will find nothing here to sustain them. Ma'at is an order, and it admits only those who are willing to be ordered by it. Those who can meet its standard will find here something that exists nowhere else on earth. Those who cannot will find nothing, and will leave of their own accord.

Those who do not seek the Gods owe us nothing. We owe them the clarity of our position and the restraint of our conduct. Beyond that, we are not their concern, and they are not ours.
